The Ultimate Surf Guide to Santa Teresa, Costa Rica

March 2025 8 min read

Santa Teresa has earned its place among the world's most coveted surf destinations—and for good reason. Located at the southern tip of Nicoya Peninsula, this small Costa Rican village offers consistent, world-class waves that attract everyone from first-time surfers to elite athletes chasing barrels and epic point breaks.

Why Santa Teresa Is a Surf Paradise

The magic of Santa Teresa lies in its geography and water patterns. The region receives consistent swell from both southwest and northwest Atlantic swells more than 70% of the year, making it one of the most reliable destinations in Central America. The peninsula's unique exposure creates multiple breaks at various levels—whether you're learning to pop up on a soft sandy beach break or hunting barrels at an exclusive reef break.

Water temperature hovers around 78-82°F year-round, meaning you'll never need a wetsuit. The landscape is a dramatic mix of dense jungle, craggy rocks, and pristine beaches, with most breaks within a short ATV ride or hike from the village center. This combination—consistent waves, warm water, and that raw tropical energy—makes Santa Teresa incredibly special.

Best Breaks for Every Level

For Beginners: Playa Carmen & Banana Beach

If you're just starting out, Playa Carmen is your destination. This sandy beach break offers long, forgiving waves that roll through with gentle power—perfect for learning to position yourself on the board and practice your pop-up. The beach is wide and safe, lined with restaurants and casual vibes. Waves here rarely get above shoulder height during most of the year, and the bottom is pure sand with no rocks or hazards.

Banana Beach, just north of Playa Carmen, offers even calmer conditions. The name comes from the curved shoreline, and the waves here are noticeably softer and slower than Carmen. If Carmen feels too pushy on a big swell day, Banana Beach is your fallback—mellow waves, warm water, perfect for confidence building.

Intermediate Progress: Santa Teresa Main & Playa Hermosa

Santa Teresa Main Beach has more punch than Playa Carmen. The break here is bowly and faster—you'll need to work on your bottom turns and carving. Swell management is critical here; on smaller days it's manageable for solid intermediates, but during the May-August green season when swell stacks up, this break becomes considerably gnarlier. Local school groups often congregate here.

Playa Hermosa sits at the northern extreme of the area, about a 20-minute drive from Playa Carmen. The beach is wide, sandy, and remarkably uncrowded. You'll find both left and right peaks breaking consistently, with palm trees shadowing the sand. This is an excellent spot to escape the Playa Carmen crowds while still getting quality wave practice. The northern location means slightly different swell patterns—often cleaner during trades.

Advanced: La Lora & Suck Rock

La Lora is a left-hand point break that stands apart: it works nearly 365 days a year due to its exposed geography. This is not a beginner wave. The break is consistent but punchy, with defined walls and barrels. La Lora requires respect and a solid understanding of point break mechanics. Best conditions arrive with southwest swell and medium-to-high tide. You'll typically paddle from the south side and work your way up the point—expect to share the lineup with serious locals.

Suck Rock is the region's ultimate test piece. This expert-only right-hand point break sits on a flat rock shelf and produces some of the most powerful barrels in Central America. Access requires overhead-minimum swell, ideally from the south or southwest, and the timing must align with higher tides. The wave retracts deeply into the point, creating perfect barrel sections for those who know what they're doing. This is a wave for surfers with years of barrel experience; the rock shelf leaves zero margin for error.

Beyond the Main Breaks: Mal Pais Reef

South of Santa Teresa, the Mal Pais reef breaks offer heavy, technical waves for experienced surfers. These are solid swell events rather than everyday waves, and the sharp reef demands respect. Snorkel trips out to the breaks are popular among surf photographers and hard-chargers chasing double-overhead power.

Seasonal Patterns & When to Go

Understanding seasonality transforms your trip. Santa Teresa has two distinct seasons for surfing:

  • Dry Season (November–April): More consistent trade winds create cleaner conditions. Swell is generally smaller and more manageable. This is prime time for beginners and intermediates; you'll get cleaner peaks, lighter offshore winds, and the most crowded lineup. High tourist season means accommodations and restaurants book early.
  • Green Season (May–August): More frequent rain, but larger Atlantic swells funnel into the peninsula. Waves can double in size, creating excellent opportunities for intermediate and advanced surfers. Winds tend to be lighter and more variable, and the lineup is significantly less crowded. Accommodation prices drop considerably.

Late September and October represent shoulder season—unpredictable swells and frequent rain, but occasional magic days with fewer people around.

"The single best piece of advice? Don't chase swells—chase the season that matches your level. Beginners in November, intermediate surfers in May, advanced athletes hunting barrels in July."

Practical Information for Surfing Santa Teresa

Board Rentals & Schools

You don't need to fly with your board. Multiple shops around Playa Carmen rent quality soft-tops ($15–25 per day) and fiberglass boards ($20–35 per day). Many also run beginner lessons (typically $45–65 per person, including a board). Local instructors know the best daily conditions and can guide you to the right break based on your level and the current swell.

Getting Around

Most breaks require an ATV or 4WD vehicle to access. Rent an ATV ($50–70 per day) from one of several shops on the main road—they'll provide maps of break locations and current conditions. Driving at night is not recommended due to rough roads and potential encounters with local wildlife. Many accommodations include parking and easy ATV access.

Crowds & Community

Playa Carmen and Santa Teresa Main get busy in high season, particularly mid-morning when tourists hit the water. La Lora and Suck Rock remain relatively uncrowded due to their expertise requirements. The local surfing community is welcoming; respect the pecking order at point breaks and donate to local beach cleanups.

Pro Tip

Bring a rash guard—not for warmth, but sun protection. The tropical sun reflects off the water and is deceptively intense. Local surfers spend more time treating burned shoulders than dealing with cold water.

Beyond the Waves

Surfing isn't the only reason to visit Santa Teresa. The village offers yoga studios, exceptional restaurants (Koji's sushi, Katana, The Bakery), and easy access to hikes like the Montezuma Falls. Between swells, explore the jungle, paddleboard, or simply rest in a private villa with jungle and ocean views.

Final Thoughts

Santa Teresa rewards both the curious beginner and the obsessive shredder. The breaks are varied enough that you can improve dramatically in a single week, and consistent enough that you'll want to come back year after year. Pick your season, respect the waves and the locals, and prepare for some of the best surfing of your life.

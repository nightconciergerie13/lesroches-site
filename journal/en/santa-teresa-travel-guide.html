Santa Teresa, Costa Rica: The Complete Travel Guide

March 2025 10 min read

Santa Teresa feels like a discovery rather than a destination. This small coastal village, perched at the southern tip of Costa Rica's Nicoya Peninsula, remains relatively untouched by mass tourism despite its growing reputation. It's a place where the jungle meets the Pacific with genuine magic—where sunrise hikes through cloud forest lead to hidden waterfalls, evening walks along the beach reveal bioluminescent creatures, and the pace of life still revolves around tides and sunset.

How to Get There

Santa Teresa isn't quite as direct as flying into San José, which is part of its charm. There are three main routes, each with advantages:

Ferry + Road (Most Popular)

This is the classic route favored by most visitors. Drive from San José via Highway 27 to Puntarenas, then take the ferry across the Gulf of Nicoya from Puntarenas to Paquera. The ferry typically takes 80 minutes, and you'll need to reserve in advance during high season—spots fill quickly. From Paquera, it's another 70–90 minutes of driving through mountain roads to reach Santa Teresa. Total travel time: 4–6 hours depending on traffic. The journey is spectacular, offering views of rural Costa Rica that most tourists never see.

Domestic Flight (Fastest)

SANSA Airlines operates direct flights from San José to Cobano (the nearest airstrip to Santa Teresa), taking approximately 30 minutes. As of October 2023, this replaced the older Tambor airstrip. Flights cost around $110 USD per person, with 1–2 departures daily depending on season. This option is ideal if you value time over budget, and eliminates the long drive and ferry wait.

Public Bus

Costa Rica's public bus system is surprisingly efficient but requires patience. Buses depart from Terminal TIG in San José at 6 a.m. and take 6–7 hours. While economical, the experience can be intense, with the journey covering winding mountain roads. This is a genuine Costa Rican experience, though most visitors prefer the comfort of the ferry or flight.

Travel Tip

Don't drive at night. Mountain roads to Santa Teresa are poorly lit and potentially dangerous after dark. If you're driving yourself, arrange to arrive by late afternoon. Many visitors hire private drivers ($150–250 USD) for a safer, more comfortable experience.

When to Visit

Santa Teresa's climate divides neatly into two distinct seasons, each with character:

Dry Season: November–April

Clear skies, morning sun, and minimal rain define these months. It's peak tourist season—hotels book weeks in advance, and beaches are lively. The air is crisp, hikes are comfortable, and sunsets are predictable. If you're planning activities like the Montezuma Falls hike, this is when to come. However, expect higher prices and larger crowds, especially December–January.

Green Season: May–August

Don't let the name fool you—it rains, but intermittently. Mornings are often clear, afternoon showers cool the air, and the landscape explodes with color. Prices drop 20–30%, restaurants have tables available, and the lineup at surf breaks thins considerably. The jungle comes alive with wildlife: howler monkeys call throughout the day, birds are more active, and the experience feels more authentic. If you're a traveler rather than a tourist, this is magical.

September–October represents shoulder season: unpredictable, occasionally wet, but occasionally perfect. Book accordingly.

Where to Eat & Drink

Koji's

The gold standard for sushi in the region. Koji's occupies a charming garden space with atmospheric lighting, and the quality is exceptional—think pristine fish, creative rolls, and their famous warm sake. Reservations are essential in high season. The experience is upscale-casual, with prices reflecting the quality (mains $18–28).

Katana

Equally excellent to Koji's but at slightly lower price points. The vibe is more casual, almost hidden-gem feeling, and the food quality never wavers. If you can't get into Koji's, Katana delivers the same caliber meal in a more relaxed setting.

The Bakery

Start your mornings here. Excellent pastries, outstanding coffee, and genuine breakfast dishes—think eggs, avocado toast done right, and fresh orange juice. It's become a gathering spot for locals and visitors alike. Arrive early; it fills quickly.

Eat Street

A casual food court with four quality vendors serving everything from tacos to fresh fish plates. It's perfect for quick lunch between activities, with generous portions at $10–20. No reservation needed, genuine local energy.

"The restaurant scene in Santa Teresa punches well above its size. You'll find exceptional sushi, excellent seafood, and surprising international cuisine—all without the pretension of resort towns."

What to Do

Montezuma Falls

A short 45-minute drive leads to a trailhead where a 20-minute hike through jungle takes you to spectacular cascading waterfalls. The main pool is deep and cold—perfect for swimming after the hike. Wear proper footwear; the rocks are slippery. Sunrise hikes reward you with fewer crowds and wildlife sightings.

Surf

Playa Carmen, Santa Teresa Main, and La Lora offer waves for all levels. Board rentals and lessons are readily available. Spend a day or a week—the breaks are consistent enough for rapid improvement.

Yoga & Wellness

Several studios offer drop-in classes for $12–15. Whether you're a dedicated yogi or just stretching after activities, the combination of jungle setting and qualified instructors makes classes memorable.

ATV Tours

Explore hidden trails, secret beaches, and jungle paths with a local guide. Rentals run $50–70 per day, and guided tours offer better access to areas you wouldn't find alone.

Tortuga Island

Full-day snorkeling and beach excursions depart from nearby docks. The journey includes excellent snorkeling, white sand beaches, and often a beachside BBQ lunch. It's touristy but genuinely worthwhile.

Curu Wildlife Reserve

Just an hour away, this protected reserve is home to monkeys, coatis, exotic birds, and reptiles. Guided hikes offer excellent wildlife spotting, particularly in early morning. Entry is approximately $30 with guide.

Horseback Riding

Sunset rides along the beach are iconic. Local guides arrange trips ($40–60 including guide) that cater to all experience levels.

Butterfly Garden & Brewery

The Butterfly Brewing Company combines artisanal craft beer with a live butterfly garden—a pleasant few hours blending nature and cold beer.

Tide Pools at Mar Azul

South of town, tide pools reveal starfish, sea urchins, and tiny fish trapped in crystal-clear water. Best visited at low tide.

Practical Information

Money & Banking

Only two ATMs exist in the village, and both charge steep international fees. Bring cash in Costa Rican colones (exchange in San José before leaving). Credit cards work at restaurants and shops, but cash is king for small transactions. Consider USD; it's widely accepted.

Communication

Most restaurants don't answer phone calls. Use WhatsApp for reservations—it's the standard communication method. Cell service is reliable; international plans work well.

Transportation

Rent an ATV ($50–70/day) or car ($40–60/day) for complete freedom. Taxis exist but are expensive for longer distances. Many accommodations arrange pickups from the airport or ferry.

Shopping & Supplies

Limited supermarkets serve basic needs. Stock up on essentials in Puntarenas before the ferry if staying remote. The village has pharmacies, a small hospital, and a few shops, but selection is limited.

Wildlife Note

Santa Teresa sits in one of the world's Blue Zones—areas with exceptional longevity and health. The Nicoya Peninsula's residents regularly live to 100 with vigor. Locals attribute it to the peaceful pace, fresh local food, and strong community bonds.

Seasons Impact Everything

High season (December–January) means booked restaurants and higher prices. Many establishments close during low season (September–October) for maintenance. Plan accordingly.

Final Thoughts

Santa Teresa rewards those who take the time to get there. It's not a quick weekend escape from San José—it's a deliberate choice to step outside conventional tourism. The village remains authentic precisely because it's inconvenient to reach. You'll find travelers here, not tourists. You'll have conversations with locals. You'll discover restaurants nobody else knows about. And you'll understand why people return year after year, always finding something new in this beautiful, unhurried corner of Costa Rica.
